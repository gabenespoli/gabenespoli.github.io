Musicians Show Enhanced Neural Synchronization at Multiple Timescales

Gabriel A Nespoli, Paolo Ammirante, Frank A Russo

Abstract

Auditory events can be considered to have spectral energy at both short and long timescales. Energy at short timescales determines the psychological phenomena of pitch and timbre, for example, and energy at long timescales determines the relationships between events, potentially giving rise to the perception of a pulse. There has been recent interest in neural oscillations and entrainment (Nozaradan et al., 2011), whereby populations of neurons synchronize their firing with external stimuli, such as auditory events. Presumably it is through this synchronization that one is able to track periodic stimuli. Neural synchronization at both timescales can be measured using electroencephalography (EEG), and it has been found that the spectrum of the EEG signal closely matches that of the stimulus (Bidelman et al., 2011; Nozaradan et al., 2011). For musicians, who in their daily life are interested in perceiving subtle differences in pitch and pulse, have been shown to better synchronize with energy at short timescales, exemplifying a larger peak at the fundamental frequency (f0) of stimuli; a similar test for long timescales has not been investigated. In the current study, EEG was measured from musicians and nonmusicians while they listened to an isochronous train of tones. The isochronous train contains energy at both short and long timescales (corresponding to pitch and pulse, respectively). Neural synchronization at both timescales was found to be more robust in musicians. Additionally, the extent of synchronization correlated with behavioural measures of pitch and pulse perception. These findings indicate that the experience-dependent plasticity observed in musicians manifests itself at multiple cortical levels corresponding to oscillations at different timescales present in music.
